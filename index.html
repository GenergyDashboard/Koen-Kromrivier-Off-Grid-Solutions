Best Solution: x3.0 Solar + 200 kWh Additional Battery (255 kWh Total)

The recommended year-round off-grid configuration is tripling the solar array to ~48 kWp and adding 200 kWh of battery storage (255 kWh total).

This was determined by running an hourly energy-balance simulation across a full synthetic year. The simulation uses the five key data columns — Production Power, Consumption Power, Battery Power, Grid Power, and SoC — from 1,730 measured hourly readings. Battery capacity (55 kWh) was verified from 70 discharge cycles, and round-trip efficiency (92%) was measured from 2,543 kWh charged vs 2,341 kWh discharged.

For months without measured data, production is scaled using Western Cape GHI irradiation ratios (3.0 kWh/m²/day in June to 7.5 in January), and consumption is adjusted upward 15–20% for winter heating and lighting.

The critical constraint is winter: June production drops to ~25.5 kWh/day while consumption rises to ~71.4 kWh/day — a 46 kWh/day deficit sustained for weeks. At x3.0 solar, even June production reaches ~76.5 kWh/day, and the 255 kWh battery bridges overcast stretches. June clears with just 18 kWh of excess — the tightest margin in the year.

The 21,131 kWh of annual excess could offset water heating or future EV charging. For summer-only off-grid with grid backup in winter, a far smaller upgrade of x1.3 solar + 80 kWh battery (135 kWh total) achieves 100%.

📝 Key Sizing Drivers

Winter deficit is the dominant constraint. Production drops to ~25 kWh/day in June while consumption rises to ~71 kWh/day — a 46 kWh/day gap that persists for weeks. No amount of battery alone can bridge a season of structural underproduction.

Battery-only upgrades plateau at ~63%. Annual production (15,113 kWh) covers only 66% of consumption (22,760 kWh), so even 200 kWh of extra battery barely moves the needle year-round.

Night load sets the battery floor. 30.7 kWh average overnight draw (14+ hours at ~2.6 kW) means the battery must cover a full night plus reserves for cloudy follow-up days.

Summer vs winter sizing are very different problems. Summer worst cases are isolated bad days (the 5-day 103.8 kWh deficit streak). Winter is a sustained structural deficit requiring the solar array itself to produce enough on clearer days to recharge through overcast stretches.

❄️ Estimated Winter Challenge

Winter months (Jun–Aug) are estimated to produce only 25–32 kWh/day while consuming 68–71 kWh/day. This creates a sustained deficit of 36–46 kWh/day that persists for weeks rather than days.

Unlike summer where worst cases are isolated bad days or short streaks, winter's challenge is chronic, not episodic. A battery can bridge a few bad summer days, but it cannot bridge an entire season where production structurally falls short.

This is why the year-round system requires x3.0 solar — tripled production (~76–96 kWh/day even in winter) creates enough surplus on clearer days to recharge the battery and carry through overcast stretches.

How These Findings Were Calculated

Step 1: Extract System Parameters from Raw Data

The Excel file contains 1,730 hourly records with 13 columns. The five key columns used are Production Power (solar output in kW), Consumption Power (load in kW), Battery Power (negative = charging, positive = discharging), Grid Power (positive = importing), and SoC (state of charge, 0–100%).

Battery capacity was determined by identifying 70 significant discharge runs where SoC dropped by more than 10%. For each run, capacity = energy_discharged ÷ (SoC_drop / 100). The median across all 70 runs was 55.0 kWh (range: 52.6–61.2 kWh).

Round-trip efficiency was calculated from total energy flows: 2,543 kWh charged into the battery vs 2,341 kWh discharged = 92.0% actual efficiency.

Solar array size was estimated from the peak observed production hour: 15.9 kW, suggesting a ~16 kWp array.

Step 2: Calibrate the Simulation Model

A simplified hourly energy-balance model was built: for each hour, compute net = production − consumption. If net is positive, charge the battery (capped at 99% SoC, accounting for efficiency losses). If net is negative, discharge the battery (floored at 12% SoC). Any remaining shortfall counts as grid purchase.

The model was calibrated by testing efficiency parameters from 90% to 100% against the actual 365 kWh of grid purchases in the data. A 95% effective efficiency best reproduced reality (simulated: 373 kWh, error: ±2.2%). This is higher than the actual 92% because the hourly model overestimates grid usage — the real inverter optimizes power flows within each hour (sub-hourly), while our model only sees the hourly averages.

Note on grid hours: The actual data shows 359 hours with grid purchases, but 55% of those are tiny amounts (<0.1 kW) caused by brief within-hour transitions. Our model simulates 157 grid hours because it can't capture these micro-transitions. The total energy (kWh) is accurate; the hour count is an undercount.

Step 3: Estimate Winter Production

The measured data only covers December–March (summer in the Southern Hemisphere). To estimate year-round performance, monthly solar production is scaled using Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) values for the Western Cape region at ~32.4°S latitude:

Western Cape GHI — June receives only 40% of January's irradiation

A production-to-irradiation ratio was calculated from the four measured months: avg daily production ÷ GHI = 8.50 kWh per kWh/m²/day. This ratio encodes the panel size, orientation, shading, and inverter characteristics. It's then applied to each month's GHI to estimate production.

Consumption adjustments: Winter consumption is estimated 15–20% higher than the measured summer average (57.6 kWh/day) to account for heating, lighting, and shorter days. Shoulder months (April, September, October) use 5–8% uplift.

Daily variability is modelled using a normal distribution (σ = 0.30 for production, 0.15 for consumption), seeded for reproducibility. This creates realistic day-to-day fluctuations including simulated cloudy days and high-demand days.

Step 4: Run Scenario Simulations

The calibrated model is run across the full synthetic year (8,760 hours) for every combination of solar multiplier (x1.0 to x3.5) and battery addition (0 to 500 kWh). For each configuration, the simulation tracks: total grid energy needed, number of grid hours, total excess solar, and monthly breakdowns.

The simulation logic for each hour:

net = production × solar_multiplier − consumption
if net > 0: charge battery (capped at 99% SoC × efficiency)
if net < 0: discharge battery (floored at 12% SoC × efficiency)
shortfall: any unmet demand → counted as grid purchase

The minimum configuration achieving 0 kWh grid at each solar level is extracted as the threshold for 100% off-grid. For year-round, the minimum is x3.0 solar + 200 kWh extra battery (255 kWh total). For summer-only, it's x1.3 solar + 80 kWh extra battery (135 kWh total).

Why x3.0 + 200 kWh specifically? Because June is the bottleneck. At x3.0, June daily production rises from 25.5 to 76.5 kWh — just enough to cover 71.4 kWh consumption with a slim margin. The 255 kWh battery bridges the worst overcast stretches where even tripled solar falls short. June clears the simulation with just 18 kWh of excess — the tightest month.

⚠️ Limitations & Assumptions

Winter estimates are modelled, not measured. The GHI-scaling approach gives reasonable estimates but cannot capture local shading, panel degradation, or unusual weather patterns. Actual winter data would significantly improve confidence.

Hourly resolution loses sub-hourly detail. The real inverter manages power flows within each hour more efficiently than our model assumes. The calibrated 95% efficiency parameter compensates for this, but edge-case behaviour may differ.

Consumption seasonality is assumed. Winter consumption is estimated at 15–20% higher than summer. Actual winter usage patterns (heating type, occupancy, etc.) could differ significantly.

Battery degradation not modelled. Over 10+ years, battery capacity will decline. A system sized tight to 100% today may fall short in later years. Building in margin (e.g., Option B at x3.0 + 150 kWh reaching 99.9%) provides a practical buffer.
